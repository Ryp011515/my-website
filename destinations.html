Deforestation

Most deforestation is occurring in tropical rainforests, because of road construction in regions that were not possible by car. Building or upgrading roads into forests makes them more accessible for exploitation. The land is only fertile for a few years. However, after that, the farmers move on to repeat the process elsewhere. Tropical forests are also cleared to make way for logging, oil palming and many more just because of selfish humans.

More carbon dioxide may be emitted into the atmosphere as a result of deforestation. This is because carbon is chemically trapped in trees' wood, and they absorb carbon dioxide from the atmosphere for photosynthesis. This carbon is released back into the atmosphere as carbon dioxide when trees are burned. Carbon dioxide builds up in the atmosphere and speeds up global warming when there are fewer trees to absorb it.

Deforestation also endangers the world's biodiversity. Tropical woods are home to numerous animal and plant species. When forests are logged or burned, many species become extinct. Some scientists believe we are already in the midst of a catastrophic extinction event.

More immediately, the removal of trees from a forest might make the soil more susceptible to erosion. This makes the remaining plants more vulnerable to fire as the forest transitions from a closed, damp environment to an open, dry one.

Deforestation can be permanent, but it is not always the case. Forests are regrowing in several places in North America as a result of conservation efforts.

Air polution

How does air pollution affect the social economy?

Air pollution leads to increased healthcare costs, loss of productivity, and diminished workforce efficiency. These factors will be affected significantly, especially in industry main countries, as people will suffocate from air diseases.

Air pollution is because of the indoor and outdoor environment caused by any chemicals and physical things. Household combustion devices, motor vehicles, and forest fires are common sources of air pollution. Outdoor and indoor air pollution cause respiratory and other diseases and are important sources of morbidity and mortality.

The WHO data show that almost all the global population (99%) breathe air that exceeds guideline limits and contains high levels of pollutants, with poor and developing countries suffering the most.

Air quality is linked to the earth’s climate and ecosystems globally. Some jobs, like policies, reduce air pollution, therefore, offer a win-win strategy for both climate and health, as well as contribute to the long cycle of climate change.
